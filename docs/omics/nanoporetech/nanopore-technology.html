How Nanopore Sequencing Works

Oxford Nanopore Technology (ONT) is fundamentally different from Illumina sequencing. Rather than sequencing by synthesis with fluorescent signals, nanopore sequencing detects changes in ionic current as a DNA or RNA strand passes through a protein pore embedded in a synthetic membrane.

  • A protein nanopore (typically CsgG or R10.4.1) is inserted into an electrically resistant polymer membrane.
  • An ionic buffer is applied across the membrane and a constant voltage drives ions through the pore, generating a measurable baseline current.
  • When a DNA or RNA strand is threaded through the pore by a motor protein (helicase), the nucleotides occlude the pore and alter the ionic current.
  • The pattern of current disruptions — called a squiggle — is recorded over time and subsequently decoded into a nucleotide sequence by a basecaller.

Because the raw molecule itself is read, nanopore sequencing can:

  • Produce reads of any length — from hundreds of bases to millions of bases (ultra-long reads).
  • Detect native base modifications (e.g., 5mC, 5hmC, 6mA) directly from the current signal, without bisulfite conversion.
  • Sequence RNA directly, without reverse transcription.

All devices are compatible with the same library preparations; output scales with the number and type of flowcells used.

Flowcell Chemistry

ONT continuously improves pore chemistry. The most current production chemistry is R10.4.1, which features a dual-reader head that improves per-read accuracy and homopolymer resolution compared to the previous R9.4.1 chemistry.

  • R9.4.1 — widely used, mature bioinformatics tooling, ~98% modal accuracy.
  • R10.4.1 — improved accuracy (>99% modal, Q20+), better for SNV and methylation calling.

Output File Formats

Format Description
FAST5 Legacy HDF5-based format storing raw signal (will be deprecated).
POD5 Current signal format; smaller, faster I/O than FAST5.
FASTQ Standard nucleotide sequences with Phred quality scores, produced after basecalling.
BAM/UBAM Alignment or unaligned BAM; Dorado outputs unaligned BAM natively, carrying move tables and modification tags.
Summary TSV Per-read sequencing summary produced by Dorado/MinKNOW with metadata (read ID, length, quality, channel, etc.).

Advantages and Limitations

Advantages

  • Ultra-long reads: Resolve structural variants, repetitive regions, phasing across entire haplotypes, and full-length transcript isoforms.
  • Real-time sequencing: Data streams during the run; adaptive sampling can reject off-target reads on-the-fly.
  • Portability: MinION connects via USB — enables field sequencing, clinical point-of-care, and space missions.
  • Native methylation detection: No chemical conversion required; 5mC, 5hmC, and 6mA called simultaneously with sequence.
  • Direct RNA: Sequence RNA without converting to cDNA, preserving poly-A tail length and base modifications.

Limitations

  • Per-read error rate: Although Q20+ is now achievable with R10.4.1 + SUP basecalling, raw error rates are higher than Illumina for equivalent cost.
  • Homopolymers: Long runs of the same base remain challenging, though R10.4.1 improves this significantly.
  • Cost per base: At low throughput, cost per base is higher than short-read sequencing.
  • Computational requirements: SUP basecalling is GPU-intensive.

Quality Scores

ONT uses the same Phred/Q score convention as Illumina:

\[Q = -10 \log_{10} P\]

where \(P\) is the probability of an incorrect base call.

Q score Accuracy Error rate
Q10 90% 1 in 10
Q20 99% 1 in 100
Q30 99.9% 1 in 1,000
Q40 99.99% 1 in 10,000

With R10.4.1 flowcells and Dorado SUP model, modal read accuracy is typically Q20–Q25. Consensus accuracy (e.g., Medaka polishing) can exceed Q50.
